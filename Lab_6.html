Home Run Rate

Let’s first look at one specific year and see what the homerun distribution looked like that year. We will look at 2019, the year where total homeruns in MLB were at its highest

mlb_2019 = mlb[mlb["year"] == 2019]
mlb_2019 = mlb_2019.sort_values("homeruns")


plt.figure(figsize=(8,5))
y = np.arange(len(mlb_2019))
plt.plot(mlb_2019["homeruns"].values, y, 'o')
plt.yticks(y, mlb_2019["team_name"].values)
plt.xlabel("Home Runs Hit")
plt.ylabel("Team")
plt.title("Home Runs Hit in 2019")
plt.text(mlb_2019["homeruns"].iloc[-1], y[-1], "  Top", va="center")
plt.text(mlb_2019["homeruns"].iloc[0],  y[0],  "  Bottom", va="center")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Currently, this graph is not the most readable. There are too many names on the y-axis, and it’s hard to see what teams correspond to what dot. One interesting observation to make is that besides for the top and bottom five teams or so, most teams hit around the same number of home runs in 2019. It might be interesting to instead look at the teams that had bigger deviations from the mean.

top_5 = mlb_2019.nlargest(5, 'homeruns')
bottom_5 = mlb_2019.nsmallest(5, 'homeruns')

mlb_2019_extreme = pd.concat([bottom_5, top_5]).sort_values("homeruns")

plt.figure(figsize=(8,5))
y = np.arange(len(mlb_2019_extreme))
plt.plot(mlb_2019_extreme["homeruns"].values, y, 'o')
plt.yticks(y, mlb_2019_extreme["team_name"].values)
plt.xlabel("Home Runs Hit")
plt.ylabel("Team")
plt.title("Home Runs Hit in 2019")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Now it is much clearer to see what teams dominated home run hitting in 2019, and what teams fell short

Comparing Home Runs from Previous Years

We now know what five teams hit the least amount of home runs in 2019. One thing we could check is to see if they have improved. Let’s use a slope chart for that

bottom_5_2019 = (
    mlb[mlb['year'] == 2019]
    .nsmallest(5, 'homeruns')
)

teams_bottom_2019 = bottom_5_2019['team_name']

bottom_5_2020 = mlb[
    (mlb['year'] == 2020) &
    (mlb['team_name'].isin(teams_bottom_2019))
]

comparison = pd.concat([
    bottom_5_2019.assign(year=2019),
    bottom_5_2020.assign(year=2020)
]).sort_values(['team_name', 'year'])
# Slopegraph
slope_df = comparison.pivot(
    index='team_name',
    columns='year',
    values='homeruns'
)

plt.figure(figsize=(7, 5))

for team in slope_df.index:
    plt.plot(
        [2019, 2020],
        slope_df.loc[team, [2019, 2020]],
        marker='o'
    )
    # Team label near 2020 point
    plt.text(
        2020.02,
        slope_df.loc[team, 2020],
        team,
        va='center'
    )

plt.xticks([2019, 2020])
plt.xlabel("Season")
plt.ylabel("Home Runs")
plt.title("Home Runs by Team: 2019 vs 2020")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Not only is this graph messy, but it also likely doesn’t tell us much. The reason that runs went so far down in 2020 compared to 2019 was not some massive drop off in offensive production, it was due to a global pandemic cutting the season to only 60 games. Instead, let’s see what happens when we compare with the year prior, 2018.

bottom_5_2018 = mlb[
    (mlb['year'] == 2018) &
    (mlb['team_name'].isin(teams_bottom_2019))
]

comparison = pd.concat([
    bottom_5_2018.assign(year=2018),
    bottom_5_2019.assign(year=2019)
]).sort_values(['team_name', 'year'])

# Slopegraph
slope_df = comparison.pivot(

)

Now we can see a bigger trend. San Fransisco moved up massively during the 2019 season, at least compared from the other bottom tier teams. We can also see a general increase in homeruns for every team.

Tracking Wins Per Year

Let’s now try to track how many wins per year each team gets. We can do this using a small multiples chart, allowing for a detailed view of multiple teams at the same time. In order to limit our search a bit, we will only be looking at teams in the National League

nl = mlb[
    (mlb['league_id'] == 'NL') &
    (mlb['year'].between(2010, 2019))
]

teams = sorted(nl['team_name'].unique())
n_teams = len(teams)
n_cols = 4
n_rows = int(np.ceil(n_teams / n_cols))

fig, axes = plt.subplots(
    n_rows,
    n_cols,
    figsize=(14, 3 * n_rows),
    sharex=True,
    sharey=True
)

axes = axes.flatten()

for ax, team in zip(axes, teams):
    team_df = nl[nl['team_name'] == team]

    ax.plot(
        team_df['year'],
        team_df['wins'],
        marker='o'
    )

    ax.set_title(team, fontsize=10)
    ax.set_xlim(2010, 2019)
    ax.set_ylim(50, 110)
    ax.grid(alpha=0.3)

    for ax in axes[len(teams):]:
        ax.axis('off')

fig.suptitle(
    "National League Team Wins (2010–2019)",
    fontsize=16,
    y=1.02
)

fig.supxlabel("Season")
fig.supylabel("Wins")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

This creates a chart that is very easy to see the historical trends of indivual teams. There are two teams where their charts stop suddenly in the middle of the decade. For the Houston Astros, they change to the American League after the 2012 season, and the Florida Marlins rebrand to the Miami Marlins the year prior. This explains the gaps in these charts.

Errors per game

The final thing we will look at is how many errors a team makes per game. Because the error amount can fluctuate greatly, we will also normalize our data to keep it consistant.

mlb['errors_z'] = (
    mlb['errors'] - mlb['errors'].mean()
) / mlb['errors'].std()

errors_2019 = mlb[mlb['year'] == 2019]
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))

plt.bar(
    errors_2019['team_name'],
    errors_2019['errors_z']
)

plt.xticks(rotation=90)
plt.xlabel("Team")
plt.ylabel("Errors per Game")
plt.title("MLB Team Errors per Game (2019)")

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Discussion

Overall, all of these charts allow for easy comparisions along a variaty of different metrics. Dot Plots allow for comparisions between many teams and makes it easy to see what a teams rank is among the rest. Using slope charts can help compare teams with each other from different years, such as how we compared home runs over a two year span to see both the indivual team increase, as well as the wider league trend. Looking at small multiples chart makes it very easy to see each indivual teams statistics which may otherwise be clumped up by other teams lines or dots. Finally, normalization helps us consider how far apart teams are compared to others.
